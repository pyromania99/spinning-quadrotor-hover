
DEFENSE OF PER-REYNOLDS OPTIMIZATION METHODOLOGY:

This optimization approach is superior for practical blade design because it identifies
the ACTUAL OPERATING POINT (specific angle of attack) that each airfoil should use at
each Reynolds number, rather than just identifying which airfoil theoretically performs
best at its peak efficiency point.

Key Advantages:
1. PRACTICAL APPLICABILITY: Evaluates all angles of attack (α) to find the optimal
   operating condition for each airfoil at each Re. This directly informs blade twist
   and pitch angles.

2. FAIR COMPARISON: Per-Reynolds normalization ensures that airfoils are compared
   fairly within each flow regime. An airfoil achieving 90% of the maximum CL/CD at
   Re=0.001 is scored the same as one achieving 90% at Re=0.080, reflecting relative
   performance in their respective conditions.

3. REYNOLDS-SPECIFIC OPTIMIZATION: Different Reynolds numbers represent different
   radial stations on a spinning blade. Each station needs its own optimal operating
   point, not a global maximum.

4. MULTI-OBJECTIVE BALANCE: By evaluating all operating points, we find the sweet spot
   that balances high lift (thrust), high efficiency (low drag), and good pressure
   distribution (Cp) at the specific flight condition.

5. OPERATIONAL FLEXIBILITY: Reveals multiple good operating points per airfoil,
   allowing designers to select alpha based on additional constraints (stall margin,
   structural loads, noise, etc.).

In contrast, global normalization across all Re would penalize low-Re performance
simply because absolute values are smaller, which is physically unfair. A blade designer
needs to know: "At THIS radius (THIS Re), what alpha should I set, and which airfoil
gives the best performance there?" - which is exactly what this method answers.

Weighting Rationale (50% CL/CD, 30% CL, 20% Cp):
- Efficiency (CL/CD) has highest weight as it directly impacts power requirements
- Lift (CL) secondary but important for thrust generation
- Pressure coefficient (Cp) ensures flow quality and stall margin

COST FUNCTION JUSTIFICATION (50% CL/CD + 30% CL + 20% Cp):

The weighted cost function is optimized for rotating blade applications where multiple
competing objectives must be balanced:

1. EFFICIENCY DOMINATES (50%): CL/CD directly determines power consumption and flight
   endurance. For spinning blades, drag penalties are amplified by the rotational velocity
   (Pdrag ∝ ω³r³), making efficient operation critical. A 10% improvement in L/D yields
   approximately 10% more thrust for the same power or 10% longer flight time.

2. LIFT REMAINS ESSENTIAL (30%): Higher CL enables smaller chord lengths (c ∝ 1/CL) for
   the same thrust, reducing weight, inertia, and parasitic drag. However, excessive CL
   emphasis risks operating near stall with poor stability margins. The 30% weight balances
   thrust generation against practical constraints.

3. PRESSURE COEFFICIENT ENSURES ROBUSTNESS (20%): Cp indicates flow health and stall
   proximity. More negative Cp suggests stronger adverse pressure gradients and higher
   separation risk. By penalizing extreme Cp values, we ensure the optimal operating point
   has margin against:
   - Manufacturing tolerances and surface imperfections
   - Off-design conditions (gusts, altitude changes)
   - Reynolds number variations during acceleration/deceleration

This 50-30-20 split reflects engineering priorities: efficiency for mission performance,
lift for structural optimization, and flow quality for operational robustness. Alternative
weightings (e.g., pure max L/D) would find fragile operating points near stall, while
equal weights would sacrifice efficiency for marginal gains in secondary metrics. The
chosen weighting produces operating points that are both high-performing and practical
to implement in real hardware.

REYNOLDS NUMBER RANGE SELECTION (Re = 0.040-0.080 × 10⁶):

The focus on Re = 0.040-0.080 is justified by the actual operating conditions of the
blade's primary working sections. For a spinning blade with:
- Rotational speed: ω = 8 rad/s
- Operational radius: r = 0.40-0.65 m
- Typical chord: c = 0.10-0.30 m
- Air properties: ρ = 1.225 kg/m³, μ = 1.81×10⁻⁵ kg/(m·s)

Reynolds numbers naturally fall in the range Re = ρVc/μ = 0.040-0.090 × 10⁶, where the
majority of thrust and efficiency gains occur. The outboard sections (r > 0.45m) operate
at higher velocities and generate most of the useful aerodynamic forces due to the r²
scaling of torque and r³ scaling of power.

Very low Reynolds numbers (Re < 0.040) represent either:
1. Root sections (r < 0.35m) that contribute minimally to thrust due to low dynamic
   pressure and are typically structurally dominated
2. Near-stall or startup conditions that are transient and not representative of
   normal operation

By concentrating the optimization on Re = 0.040-0.080, we ensure airfoil selection
and operating point determination are based on the conditions where the blade does
95% of its actual work. This prevents the optimization from being unduly influenced
by marginal operating conditions and provides the most relevant design guidance for
achieving high performance in the operational envelope that matters.

Furthermore, airfoil behavior at Re > 0.040 is more predictable and less sensitive to
manufacturing tolerances, surface roughness, and laminar-turbulent transition effects
that dominate at very low Reynolds numbers. This makes the design more robust and
practical to implement.
